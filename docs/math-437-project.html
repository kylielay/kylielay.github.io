Stats Down South (Park)

Summary

My objective was to predict the average rating of a South Park episode on IMDb. Ultimately, I found that the k-nearest neighbors model I came up with doesn’t have any practical uses, since both the response and the predictors are equally accessible on the same website.

Furthermore, since the data on IMDb comes from a voluntary sample, the response variable (the average rating of an episode) tended to be very high. This can be seen in the histogram below showing data from the training set, where the x-axis shows the average rating out of 10 stars and the heights of the bars show the number of episodes with that average rating. We can see that the histogram is centered at about 8 stars, with only a few episodes getting an average rating below 7 stars.

Motivation and Context

I am investigating whether or not the average rating of a South Park episode on IMDb can be predicted using data about the episode such as the number of votes it got on IMDb. Specifically, what I am trying to predict is the average of the ratings an episode received among all of the users that gave it a rating from 1 to 10 stars. The reason I chose to investigate this is because the average rating of an episode seemed like the most appropriate metric for measuring how well an episode did among audiences, which can be useful in determining how future episodes should be written.

Data Description

I will be using data from the SouthPark_IMDB_Data data set. Information about the data can be found here. This data was collected by Amalan Mahendran, or Amalan-ConStat on GitHub. The information in the data set was taken from South Park’s IMDb website and seems to have been collected to be used in other users’ statistical analysis. One observation represents one episode of South Park. There are 326 observations and 8 variables in this data set.

library(SouthParkRshiny)

Data Wrangling

I split the data by putting episodes from 2013 or earlier into the training set, and episodes from after 2013 into the test set. This left me with a reasonable amount of data in both sets (248 observations in the training set and 78 observations in the test set). I did the split based on the year the episodes were aired because this puts the earliest episodes in the training set and the latest episodes in the test set, which prevents our analysis later from being biased by data “from the future.” Since the data set is very clean and has few variables, no further data wrangling was required.

sp_train <- SouthPark_IMDB_Data[which(SouthPark_IMDB_Data$Year <= 2013),]
sp_test <- SouthPark_IMDB_Data[which(SouthPark_IMDB_Data$Year > 2013),]

Exploratory Data Analysis

In this section, my goal was to determine which variables in the data set would be most appropriate to include in my model.

First, I will explore the AverageRating variable, since this will be my response variable. This variable measures the average rating of an episode on IMDb on a scale from 1 to 10 stars. I assumed most of the ratings would come from people who had a strong opinion about the show. Furthermore, I believed that those who didn’t like the show would stop watching after a few minutes, and thus not leave a rating. Therefore, I assumed that AverageRating would be centered around 8 stars with a relatively low standard deviation. I also thought that some episodes were bound to do poorly, so I assumed the distribution of AverageRating would have a left tail.

Looking at the 5 number summary as well as the mean and standard deviation of AverageRating, we can see that my assumptions about the center and variability were mostly correct. However, comparing the median to the mean, we can see that they are very similar, which suggests that the shape of the distribution is more symmetric than I thought. In fact, even the minimum is higher than I expected.

This begs the question, how does a show as controversial as South Park get such high average ratings? If we look at the IMDb page for South Park, we do see that it tends to get an overwhelming amount of high ratings compared to low ratings. Looking at other shows on IMDb, we see that they also tend to get higher average ratings in general, so this isn’t something specific to South Park. It could be that users don’t want to have disliked shows on their profile, or that they don’t watch disliked episodes for long enough to give them a rating, but this is ultimately all speculation. However, it could be important to consider when using the average rating to measure the general reception of an episode that the information on IMDb comes from a voluntary sample. The average rating on IMDb might be higher than if we took a truly random sample of people who watched South Park and asked for their ratings.

Next, I moved on to the rest of the variables in the data set. I ignored the PrimaryTitle and OriginalTitle variables, since they basically serve as ID variables.

I suspected that the Season and Year variables might be highly correlated, since seasons are numbered in order of when they came out. To see if this was the case, I looked at a scatterplot for Season and Year. Each point represents one episode, although multiple points are stacked on top of each other because multiple episodes come out in the same season and year.

As we can see, Season and Year are almost perfectly linearly correlated, with only three of the earliest seasons spanning across two years. Thus, we don’t want to use both of these variables in our modeling due to collinearity issues, which would lead to inflated standard errors and unreliable coefficient estimates. If these variables were to be included in the model, we would have to choose between one or the other, depending on which one seems more informative for our response. However, since the Season and Year variables serve to catalog when an episode was made, and I split my data based on Year, neither of these seemed appropriate to include in my model.

The Episode variable also serves to catalog when an episode was made, and I did not believe this would yield anything interesting when looked at on its own.

Modeling

Here, I fit a k-nearest neighbors model predicting AverageRating from Episode, Runtime, and NumberOfVotes.

The way this model makes predictions for an observation is, for some positive integer k that we choose, it finds the k closest points in the training set to that observation. If our response is numerical, it predicts the average of those points’ response values, and if our response is categorical, it predicts the most common class among those points. These points may also be weighted so that points that are closer to the observation have more influence over the predicted value or class of that observation. K-nearest neighbors does not make any assumptions.

When fitting the k-nearest neighbors model below, we will tune neighbors. This represents the “k” mentioned above, or the number of closest points to an observation that the model will consider when making predictions. For lower values of neighbors, the model will have higher variance and lower bias because the average response value (or most common class) will only be calculated using a few points (possibly even only one point), so predictions will vary greatly if a new point is added. For higher values of neighbors, the model will have higher bias and lower variance because the average response value (or most common class) will be calculated using the majority (if not all) of the training data, so adding a new point will not greatly influence the predictions.

knn_model <- nearest_neighbor(
  mode = "regression",
  neighbors = tune(),
  dist_power = 2
)

knn_recipe <- recipe(
  AverageRating ~ Episode + Runtime + NumberOfVotes,
  data = nopilot_train
) |>
  step_normalize(all_numeric_predictors())

knn_wflow <- workflow() |>
  add_model(knn_model) |>
  add_recipe(knn_recipe)

# cross validation
set.seed(437)
sp_kfold <- vfold_cv(nopilot_train, v = 5, repeats = 2)

knn_grid <- expand.grid(neighbors = seq(10, 100, by = 10))

knn_tune <- knn_wflow |>
  tune_grid(
    resamples = sp_kfold,
    grid = knn_grid,
    metrics = metric_set(rmse)
  )

From the value of neighbors we chose in our cross validation, we see that we got an average RMSE of about 0.476 and a standard error of about 0.022. To know if this is good or not, let’s compare it to what we would get with the null model (k-nearest neighbors model with 247 neighbors, the largest value of neighbors possible with our data).

We see that the null model gives us an average RMSE of about 0.552 and a standard error of about 0.020. The standard error is close to what we got with our tuned model, and the average RMSE is a bit higher. Thus, our model is performing slightly better than the null model.

Conclusion

My goal was to predict the average rating of South Park episodes on IMDb. In my exploratory data analysis, I found that many of the variables in the data were not actually useful to include in my modeling. Variables that yielded interesting results were NumberOfVotes, which was found to have notable outliers, and Runtime, which was also found to have a few outliers with the majority of episodes having a runtime of 22 minutes. Using these variables as well as the AverageRating and Episode variables, I was able to fit a k-nearest neighbors model to predict the average rating of an episode. This model performed better than the null model.

As we can see, the model consistently makes poorer predictions on episodes with an average rating of less than 7 stars compared to other episodes. This makes sense considering that in the histogram of AverageRating seen in the exploratory data analysis, we saw that most episodes get an average rating above 7 stars.

However, this means that if an episode has a low average rating, we would not be able to accurately predict that using this model. Thus, this model may not be useful in actually answering the question of whether or not an episode performed well among audiences.

Data that is collected through a truly random sample, rather than through a voluntary sample as is the case on IMDb, may be more useful in creating a model that predicts the performance of an episode. Furthermore, the fact that all of the data comes from the IMDb page for South Park means that in practice, a model such as the one shown here may not be useful. This is because if we need the information from IMDb to find the number of votes for an episode in order to estimate the average rating, we could also simply look up the average rating on IMDb.
